# Abstract And Impression Poster Grammar

Use this when the user asks for abstract, impressionistic, graphic, color-block, typography-led, collage, or less realistic posters; when a title-only brief is mostly emotional or philosophical; or when a batch has drifted into too many realistic/festival-style posters.

The goal is not random abstraction. Premium abstract posters keep one readable anchor, turn the world into an emotional field, make typography part of the composition, and use print/material behavior to keep the result from becoming wallpaper.

## Core Rule

Build every abstract or impressionistic poster from five decisions:

1. **Visual anchor**: one person, eye, blade, chair, tree, throne, smoke column, letterform, object, void, or silhouette that lets the viewer read the poster.
2. **Dominant field**: one large color, texture, shadow, stage, sky, paper, tree canopy, typographic plane, or printed surface that carries the emotional pressure.
3. **Field conflict**: a clear opposition such as tiny vs huge, red vs black, blank vs crowded, soft memory vs sharp threat, flat field vs photographic object, or calm geometry vs violence.
4. **Typography role**: title as architecture, title as label, title as cut, title as stage signage, title as institutional grid, title as whisper, or title as marginalia.
5. **Material behavior**: halftone, risograph, screen print, rough ink, paper grain, rubbed texture, photographic flash, chalk, gouache, matte stock, or worn lithograph.

If any one of these is missing, the route is likely too vague.

## Abstract Fit Gate

Choose abstraction when at least one is true:

- The user explicitly asks for abstract, impressionistic, color-block, graphic, typography-led, painterly, collage, or less realistic treatment.
- The brief is title-only and carries mood, philosophy, memory, dream, time, absurdity, grief, silence, bureaucracy, power, danger, or identity more than plot.
- The subject can be compressed into one visual anchor and one emotional field.
- The strongest poster promise is a visual argument rather than a literal scene.
- A batch has become too realistic and one or two title-only prompts can defensibly support non-realistic interpretation.

Do not choose abstraction when:

- A concrete person, place, incident, profession, or documentary fact is the main source of stakes.
- The route needs a paragraph to explain.
- The result would remove the subject entirely.
- The prompt would become only "beautiful abstract background" without a poster anchor.

## Route Families

### 1. Color Field Poster

Use when emotion, pressure, power, loneliness, judgment, or violence is stronger than literal scenery.

Construction grammar:
- huge matte field of one dominant color
- tiny figure, object, or shadow as the anchor
- restrained but visible title/copy hierarchy
- surface texture that prevents flat digital fill

Prompt language:
`a vertical color-field cinematic poster, enormous textured [color] field, tiny [anchor] placed [location], long shadow or rupture, visible title typography, matte paper grain`

Avoid:
random gradients, decorative emptiness, text-free concept plate.

### 2. Symbol Minimal Poster

Use when one object can carry the whole story: blade, chair, smoke, door, eye, burned paper, evidence tag, cage, flower, ticket, or key.

Construction grammar:
- one object or mark dominates
- background is reduced to black, white, red, cream, or one material surface
- copy becomes a precise label or vertical title block

Prompt language:
`extreme minimalist symbolic poster, one [object] as the only narrative anchor, vast [field], severe title placement, rough print texture`

Avoid:
generic icons, clipart symbols, over-clever metaphors that hide the subject.

### 3. Typographic Architecture

Use when the title, brand phrase, concept word, music, law, bureaucracy, technology, or identity can become the image.

Construction grammar:
- huge letterforms become wall, road, stage, instrument, cage, smoke, cut, or horizon
- small human/object scale clarifies the stakes
- title and image are not separable

Prompt language:
`typographic architecture poster, giant [letter/title] forms become [space/object], tiny [anchor] inside the typography, strong title-as-image hierarchy, printed texture`

Avoid:
cheap 3D text, bevel effects, floating slogan blocks.

### 4. Constructivist Plane Conflict

Use for conflict, labor, politics, business tension, speed, ideology, institutional force, or direct public-message energy.

Construction grammar:
- diagonal planes, circles, rectangles, arrows implied by geometry, not stickers
- limited red/black/cream/gray or primary color palette
- bold sans-serif title integrated with the planes

Prompt language:
`constructivist graphic poster, diagonal [color] planes colliding with [shape/object], limited palette, bold sans-serif title locked to the geometry, screen-print grain`

Avoid:
corporate infographic, random arrows, propaganda cliche without subject logic.

### 5. Polish Surreal Metaphor

Use for theatre, horror, satire, revenge, moral contradiction, political subtext, psychological tension, or stories that need an oblique image.

Construction grammar:
- one unsettling central metaphor
- rough hand-rendered texture
- body fragment, eye, mask, animal/object hybrid, or impossible object
- expressive lettering that feels drawn into the poster

Prompt language:
`surreal metaphor poster in rough printed cultural-poster language, [body/object] transformed into [metaphor], expressive hand-rendered title, limited violent color field`

Avoid:
copying specific historic posters, random grotesque collage, unreadable chaos.

### 6. Risograph / Halftone Light Field

Use for memory, youth, music, sky, night, dream, spiritual distance, summer, or fragile hope.

Construction grammar:
- one or two ink colors with visible dot texture
- light burst, cloud, water, grass, face, or sky becomes a printed emotional field
- title sits as a designed block, not as a caption

Prompt language:
`risograph halftone poster, [blue/white/red/cream] ink field, blown-out [light/sky/cloud] anchor, visible dot pattern, bold printed title hierarchy`

Avoid:
generic vaporwave, soft wallpaper, illegible low-contrast text.

### 7. Theatrical Void

Use for power, performance, judgment, hierarchy, corporate stage, loneliness, social control, occult, or institutional pressure.

Construction grammar:
- black field or stage void
- bright doorways, seats, throne, curtain planes, or panels
- silhouettes used as scale and power markers
- title/copy behaves like show billing or institutional signage

Prompt language:
`theatrical void poster, black stage field with [red/magenta/white] light portals, [chair/throne/seats/silhouettes] as anchor, designed title and footer metadata`

Avoid:
event flyer, corporate conference banner, random spotlights.

### 8. Impressionistic Memory Field

Use for family, school, grief, coming of age, memory, ecology, place, nostalgia, or life passing.

Construction grammar:
- landscape, tree, rain, grass, sea, or street is treated as a memory surface
- small figures are subordinate to the field
- color is heightened but tied to feeling
- handwriting or modest typography can work when integrated

Prompt language:
`impressionistic memory poster, [tree/canopy/rain/field] becomes a vast emotional map, tiny figures at the base, heightened [color] field, tactile print grain, integrated title`

Avoid:
generic pretty landscape, stock nature photo, sentimental postcard.

## Pre-Prompt Abstract Check

Before writing the final prompt for any abstract or impressionistic route, answer:

- What is the visual anchor?
- What is the dominant field?
- What is the field conflict?
- How does typography participate in the image?
- What print or material behavior prevents wallpaper?
- Can the viewer feel the poster promise in three seconds?

If the answer is weak, revise the mechanism before generating.

## Source Grammar Policy

Use public art and poster movements as grammar, not as copying instructions:

- Polish poster tradition: surreal metaphor, rough texture, oblique cultural commentary.
- Swiss / International Typographic Style: grid, asymmetry, typography and image contrast.
- Color Field painting: large color areas as emotional structure.
- Abstract Expressionism: abstraction as strong expressive content.
- Olympic identity systems: graphic clarity, pictograms, grid discipline, public recognition.

Never ask an image model to reproduce a specific poster, logo, artist, living designer, trademark, or protected character. Translate references into composition, color, typography, material, and narrative function.
