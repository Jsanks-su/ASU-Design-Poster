# Chinese Cinematic Grammar

Use this when the subject is Chinese culture, Chinese history, wuxia, myth, folk memory, Chinese-language drama, Chinese city life, or when the user asks for "Chinese style", "guofeng", or "国风".

Do not reduce Chinese style to ink wash, calligraphy, antique costumes, scroll borders, red seals, bamboo, lanterns, or a woman in hanfu. Strong contemporary Chinese posters often work because a Chinese material, spatial system, color field, type gesture, or symbolic object becomes the whole composition.

## Core Rule

Pick one Chinese cinematic mechanism before writing the prompt:

1. **Character as force**: calligraphic strokes, seal forms, stele-rubbing marks, or title structure create the poster's movement.
2. **Space as fate**: road, gate, courtyard, palace hall, opera stage, mountain pass, bridge, wall, river bend, curtain, or screen defines destiny and pressure.
3. **Material as history**: rice paper, rubbed stone, lacquer, gold leaf, brocade, old archive, burnt paper, opera curtain, porcelain glaze, temple wood, rain-wet street.
4. **Color field as emotion**: red/black rupture, yellow/black shadow, white/black silence, gold/black ritual, blue-green mountain memory, cinnabar as wound or judgment.
5. **Tiny human vs vast structure**: a small figure under a massive gate, wave, mountain, calligraphic stroke, palace roof, shadow, or red field.
6. **Modern Chinese collision**: neon street, elevator, office, subway, night market, surveillance, karaoke room, traffic light, apartment block, or business suit collides with classical rhythm or ritual color.

## Modern Guofeng Routes

| Route | Use when | Poster move |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Minimal ink force | The story is moral, lonely, fatal, or philosophical. | One black stroke, path, shadow, or figure on white/cream space; let scale and silence carry tension. |
| Red-black cut | The story has revenge, political danger, forbidden desire, trial, sacrifice, or memory. | A red field or red wound cuts through black/gray space; keep the image simple and severe. |
| Shadow theatre | The subject involves identity, deception, martial arts, doubles, performance, or politics. | Use silhouettes, cast shadows, opera-stage depth, screen layers, or figures turned into brush marks. |
| Palace / gate / ritual space | The subject involves power, dynasty, hierarchy, court, law, or family order. | Use architecture as pressure; small humans trapped under ceremonial symmetry. |
| Mountain-water as psychology | The subject is wandering, exile, fate, memory, or spiritual distance. | Landscape is not scenery; it becomes the character's mental structure, route, or wound. |
| Material archive | The subject involves case files, history, evidence, ancestry, old power, or cultural memory. | Use rubbed stone, burned documents, legal papers, ledger grids, old maps, seals, or torn paper as the image body. |
| Contemporary guofeng | The subject is modern but culturally Chinese. | Use contemporary settings with Chinese compositional rhythm: vertical hierarchy, empty center, ritual color, reflected signage, screen divisions, or calligraphic motion. |
| Decorative epic | The subject is myth, fantasy, animation, or ceremonial spectacle. | Ornament can appear, but it must organize scale and movement; avoid wallpaper-like decoration. |

## Typography Logic

Chinese typography should be chosen by role, not by reflex:

- **Brush as image**: title strokes become wave, shadow, road, blood, wind, or structure.
- **Inscription as authority**: stele, seal, plaque, vertical carving, or archive label carries law, memory, or ritual.
- **Modern Chinese display**: strong square forms, condensed vertical type, or high-contrast printed title for contemporary drama, noir, and commercial readability.
- **Small title against vast space**: use when silence is stronger than display.
- **Bilingual hierarchy**: Chinese title can dominate cultural or historical routes; English subtitle can be small, restrained, or export-style when useful.

Avoid generic computer calligraphy. Specify the physical behavior: dry brush, broken stroke, rubbed stone, carved plaque, gold-leaf type, printed cinema title, hand-painted theatre lettering, or clean modern Chinese display type.

## Color Logic

Chinese color should carry story, not decoration:

- **Cinnabar red**: wound, authority, ritual, desire, blood, verdict, wedding/funeral counterpoint.
- **Ink black**: fate, law, shadow, silence, moral weight, hidden violence.
- **Imperial yellow / ochre**: power, age, heat, command, dryness, decay.
- **Gold leaf**: ceremony, wealth, divinity, illusion, memory; use with restraint.
- **Blue-green mountain tones**: distance, history, dream, exile, ghostly calm.
- **Rice paper / bone white**: emptiness, judgment, mourning, archive, breath.

## Anti-Stale Chinese Style

Reject these unless the user explicitly asks for a conventional costume-drama look:

- hanfu character pasted over a misty mountain background
- calligraphy title plus red seal as decoration only
- lanterns, bamboo, fans, clouds, dragons, plum blossoms, and palace roofs used as generic symbols
- overfilled fantasy ornament with no hierarchy
- ink wash that is just a gray filter
- "ancient China" without period, material, social space, or dramatic mechanism
- symmetrical palace poster where architecture is only background, not pressure

## Prompt Pattern

```text
Chinese cinematic poster direction using [route]. Image mechanism: [one mechanism from the list]. The composition is built from [space/material/color/type], with [small figure/object] placed against [vast structure/field/gesture] to create [emotional tension]. Typography behaves as [brush/image/inscription/modern display/small silence], not decorative calligraphy. Color strategy: [dominant field], [support], [accent and narrative job]. Premium contemporary Chinese pictorial poster, culturally specific, graphically disciplined, not generic costume-drama art.
```
