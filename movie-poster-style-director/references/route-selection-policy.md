# Route Selection Policy

Use this with `style-router.md`. The goal is not to maximize style variety. The goal is to choose the strongest poster interpretation for the content.

## Core Rule

Fit the content first. Variation is allowed only when it creates a defensible interpretation of the same content, not when it mechanically swaps styles.

Every route must answer:

- What is the poster saying?
- Why is this visual world the right vehicle?
- Is the route faithful, counterpoint, argument-led, material-led, or audience-led?

## Design Stances

| Stance | Use when | Poster move |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Congruent | The expected genre or subject language is already strong. | Match the emotional promise with disciplined craft: historical ink for a Song legal story, natural history dignity for wildlife, noir for crime. |
| Counterpoint | A surprising mismatch reveals the subject more sharply. | Use calm color for violence, cheerful surface for dread, clean bureaucracy for moral rot, beauty against destruction. The contrast must clarify the idea. |
| Argument-led | The poster should make a point, not just depict. | Turn the thesis into one metaphor: a bear body becomes logged forest, a legal case becomes a torn archive, revenge becomes a red cut. |
| Material-led | The medium itself carries time, culture, evidence, or memory. | Use stele rubbing, case files, newsprint, celluloid, textile, fresco, ink, map, ledger, ticket, or damaged paper as story material. |
| Audience-led | The poster needs broad instant recognition or commercial readability. | Use a legible central figure/object, strong title hierarchy, direct color contrast, and familiar genre cues without becoming a cheap template. |

## Short-Title Routing

When the user gives only a title, build a hidden shortlist of 3-5 route hypotheses:

1. a faithful or genre-congruent route
2. an argument-led metaphor route
3. a counterpoint route if it sharpens the idea
4. a material or typography-led route when title/period/evidence matters
5. a popular-readability route when the title needs immediate mass appeal

Pick the route with the clearest three-second promise. Do not pick abstraction only because it looks premium. Do not pick realism if it lacks a concrete scene, subject, or visual fact.

## Counterpoint Gate

Counterpoint is optional, not required. Use it only when all are true:

- the poster still reads as the requested subject
- the contrast reveals a sharper idea than a faithful route
- the contrast can be explained in one sentence
- color, typography, and symbol still form one coherent poster

If the counterpoint cannot pass this gate, choose the congruent route.

## Realism Gate

Use documentary or live-action realism when the brief has concrete anchors:

- specific person, place, incident, object, period detail, profession, or scene
- a desired photojournalistic, natural-history, or live-action film feeling
- a human face or body whose realism carries the stakes

If the input is title-only, realism needs one invented but precise cinematic situation. Avoid generic animal photos, stock portraits, empty glamour, or ad-like issue posters.

## Multiple Outputs

For a batch, do not force each image into a different style family. Start from the content and choose the few strongest hypotheses. Several outputs may share the same broad route when that is what the subject demands.

Acceptable batch variation when the content supports it:

- same broad route, different stance: faithful historical case file, counterpoint white procedural, argument-led blood archive
- same medium, different typography: vertical seal, diagonal cut, evidence marginalia, title-as-landscape
- same subject, different color logic: congruent muted palette, bold accent rupture, popular high-recognition contrast

Bad batch variation:

- one realism, one anime, one ink, one collage just to fill quotas
- four posters with the same metaphor, composition, and color but different surface texture
- routes that contradict the content without a clear reason

## Selection Rubric

Choose the route that scores highest on:

- three-second emotional promise
- visual specificity
- story or argument clarity
- title/copy hierarchy
- color strategy
- anti-low-design resilience
